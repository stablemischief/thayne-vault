# Skill 03: Startup Valuation Methodology
## How to Establish and Defend a Pre-Revenue Valuation

---

## The Mindset Shift (Critical)
Chris said it directly: "Don't think about it like your checking account."

Most bootstrappers think: *How much do I need to survive?*
Institutional investors think: *What is the equity worth at exit, and what percentage am I buying?*

You are not asking for a loan. You are selling a piece of a company that will be worth dramatically more in 5–7 years. The number you raise around should reflect that future value — discounted back to today — not your monthly burn rate.

---

## Pre-Revenue Valuation: How It's Actually Done

Since you have no revenue yet, valuation is based on:

**1. Team Quality & Track Record**
The most important factor at early stage. Investors back teams, not ideas.
- Chris's exits ($4B, $1B) on the Agrellus deck got immediate attention — "when you've got a team that's been in the billions, that's a different thought process"
- Your credibility: built Agfin in <6 months with 2 people; Dynamous methodology; Kowerk in parallel
- Mitch's credibility: built the bulk upload infrastructure; full-stack execution
- Advisor credibility: Chris Johnson, Tyler (Dynamous), Cole (Dynamous founder)

**2. Market Size & Timing**
- Large, clearly defined market that is underserved = higher valuation
- Agriculture: $342B TAM (combined Agrellus model), legacy systems, no dominant AI player
- Timing: AI is at the inflection point. Agriculture is "medicine 30 years ago"

**3. Comparable Transactions (Comps)**
What are similar companies being valued at?
- Vertical AI companies in early stage: typically $8M–$30M pre-money on first institutional round
- Ag-tech software companies: $10M–$50M pre-money on Seed/Series A
- Key comp: if you're selling 15% of the company for $1.5M investment → $10M pre-money valuation

**4. Defensibility / Moat**
- Proprietary data (farm-level FSA data, crop insurance data, transaction data)
- Relationships (Agrellus → 1.5M farmers; Nesvik → commodity brokerage)
- Methodology (AI-native development — you can build faster and cheaper than traditional dev shops)
- Multi-agent architecture (documented, repeatable, scalable)

## TSR (Total Shareholder Return) Framework
Chris used this in the Agrellus deck. It's critical for framing valuation.

**The Concept:**
TSR = capital appreciation + dividends. Investors compare TSR across industries to decide where to put money.

**The Agrellus/Chris Framework:**
- Pure agriculture companies: ~5% TSR (commodity-level — not attractive to sophisticated investors)
- Banks, consumer goods, media: 10–17% TSR
- Technology + media + financial services combined: 15–20%+ TSR

**Your Positioning:**
Stable Mischief is NOT an agriculture company. It's a **technology + data + financial services** company that happens to serve agriculture. By constructing the company this way:
- Your TSR profile moves from 5% to 15–17%
- Your valuation multiple improves dramatically
- You attract institutional capital that agriculture alone wouldn't attract

**How to say it in the pitch:**
"We're not an ag company. We're a vertical technology and data platform. Our revenue comes from licensing, SaaS, data monetization, and financial services — not from selling crops. That's why our TSR profile is in the financial services/technology tier, not the commodity tier."

## The 2–2.5x Rule (Chris's Framework)
"If you said you needed three, I'd say you need eight and a half."

**The formula:**
Actual operational need × 2.5 = Your raise target

| You Think You Need | Target Raise |
|---|---|
| $3M | ~$7.5–8M |
| $5M | ~$12–13M |
| $8M | ~$18–20M |

**Why:**
1. You will always need more than you think
2. Undercapitalization is the #1 startup killer
3. Raising more signals confidence and ambition — investors are attracted to scale
4. The difference between $3M and $8M in equity given up is often less than you think at a properly set valuation
5. You can always not deploy all the capital — but you can't go back for more mid-stride without signaling trouble

## Setting the Number: Chris's Process
1. **Establish your realistic internal valuation** — what you actually believe the company is worth today (be honest: team, IP, relationships, market position)
2. **Push it 25–50% higher** for the market-facing number ("we go high because we want them to say 'that seems high'")
3. **Structure your SAFE cap** at or slightly below your pushed number — early believers get a discount on that
4. **Your floor is your realistic number** — if they push back, you have room to negotiate without giving away the store

**Example:**
- Internal realistic valuation: $30M (based on team, Agfin IP, Agrellus relationship, market)
- Market-facing valuation: $50–60M
- Early SAFE cap: $40M (VIP tier gets this favorable rate)
- Institutional tier SAFE cap: $50M

## Defending the Valuation When Challenged
Chris: "If they're still in the room saying 'that seems high' — you've got a live fish."

**Framework for defending:**
- "Our valuation reflects the market opportunity, not current revenue. We're pre-revenue by design — we're building before we sell, not selling before we build."
- "The ag market hasn't had a technology company do this. First real institutional-grade vertical AI platform in this space — we're pricing accordingly."
- "Our team has built and deployed production AI in this specific domain in 6 months with 2 people. With capital, we scale the team and the output. That's the bet."
- "Our advisor bench — [Chris], [Tyler], [Cole] — represents exits in the billions and the bleeding edge of AI development. That's not a bootstrap team."

---

## What to Avoid
- Don't say "I need X to cover payroll for 18 months" — this is bootstrap thinking
- Don't anchor to what you're comfortable with — anchor to what the market supports
- Don't negotiate against yourself before the investor says anything
- Don't set valuation so low that early investors make the exit look bad (they need a 10x+ return to justify the risk)
