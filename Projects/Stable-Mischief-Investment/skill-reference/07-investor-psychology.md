# Skill 07: Investor Psychology & Deal Dynamics
## How Institutional Investors Think, What They Want, and How to Stay in the Driver's Seat

---

## The Two Worlds (Chris's Framework — Expanded)

### World 1: Bootstrapper / Angel World
- Investors: friends, family, local angels, small checks ($25K–$250K)
- What they care about: Do I trust this person? Is this interesting? Can they pull it off?
- Decision process: gut feeling, relationship-driven
- Terms: informal, often poorly structured
- Startup behavior: "I'll give you 5% for $100K" — often desperate, undervalued
- Outcome: often leads to messy cap tables, wrong partners, not enough money

### World 2: Institutional World
- Investors: VC firms, family offices, corporate VCs, Farm Credit, banks, insurance cos
- What they care about: team track record, market size, exit clarity, structure
- Decision process: formal, committee-driven, LP accountability
- Terms: professional (SAFE → priced round → board seats)
- Startup behavior: "We're raising $X at $Y cap, here are the terms" — from a position of strength
- Outcome: real capital, strategic relationships, credibility signal to future investors

**You are moving from World 1 to World 2. Everything about how you present, structure, and talk about the company changes.**

---

## What Institutional Investors Actually Optimize For

**1. Return on Investment (10x minimum thinking)**
At early stage, investors expect most companies to fail. The ones that succeed need to return 10x+ to make the math work for their portfolio. At a $50M valuation, they need to believe this company could be worth $500M+.

**2. Exit Clarity**
"What's my exit?" — Chris said this is ALL they want to know. Not the product, not the technology. Who buys this, for how much, in what timeframe?

**3. Team Execution Ability**
First-time founders with domain expertise > second-time founders with no domain knowledge. Institutional investors pattern-match on: Have these people done hard things? Have they shipped? Do they have the relationships to open doors?

**4. Market Timing**
Is this the right moment? AI in agriculture in 2026 is near-perfect timing — the tools exist, the pain is obvious, the incumbents are slow.

**5. Deal Terms**
They need clean terms, a clear cap table, and a path to their next round. Messy equity structures (too many shareholders, unclear vesting, no proper legal docs) are immediate red flags.

## The "That Seems High" Signal
Chris made this explicit. When an investor says "your valuation seems high" and they're still in the room — that's a buy signal.

**What it means:**
- They're interested enough to negotiate
- They're testing you to see if you'll fold
- They believe in the company but want better terms

**What to do:**
- Don't immediately drop your number
- Ask: "What metrics are you using to arrive at that number?" — make them explain their position
- Reframe: "We're pricing based on the market opportunity and team capability, not current revenue. What comparable would you use?"
- If pressed: "We're at the beginning of a tiered structure. There may be room in the early round at a cap that reflects your concerns."

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't apologize for your valuation
- Don't immediately concede
- Don't say "well, we could do it for less" before they push hard

## The Scarcity & Urgency Dynamic
Chris described using tiered SAFEs specifically to create legitimate urgency.

**How to deploy:**
- "We have a limited allocation in our Tier 1 SAFE at the $40M cap. Once that fills, we move to Tier 2 at $55M."
- This is not a lie if you've actually structured it this way
- It gives investors a real reason to move: "if you wait, you get worse terms"

**What makes scarcity legitimate:**
- A real cap on Tier 1 allocation (e.g., first $1.5M at Tier 1 terms)
- Real interest from other investors (mention it: "We have two other parties looking at this")
- Real timing pressure (you're moving forward regardless — they're deciding whether to be in or out)

## Types of Institutional Investors (and What They're Like)

**Venture Capital (VC)**
- Professionals managing pooled funds (limited partners = LPs)
- Looking for 10x+ returns, often in 5–10 years
- Want board seat (usually at Series A+, not SAFE)
- High volume of pitches — decision cycle: weeks to months
- Best fit: once you have traction and are raising $5M+

**Family Offices**
- Wealthy families investing their own money
- More flexible than VCs — can do unusual structures
- Often move faster, less bureaucracy
- May take a longer-term view (patient capital)
- Chris's personal network fits here

**Corporate Strategic Investors (CVCs)**
- Companies investing in startups adjacent to their business
- John Deere Ventures, Bayer Crop Science Ventures, etc.
- Slower moving but bring distribution, customers, credibility
- Often a precursor to acquisition
- Best fit: once your product is deployed

**Farm Credit / Ag Banks**
- The institutions Chris specifically called out
- They have billions under management and are actively looking for fintech solutions
- They would want the technology (licensing), not necessarily equity
- Relationship-driven — you reach them through Chris's network

**Angel Networks / High Net Worth Individuals**
- Like Adam at Nesvik — wealthy individuals who write $100K–$500K checks
- Often in via SAFE
- Don't need institutional process — can move fast
- Can become champions who introduce you to bigger investors

## The Board / Advisory Compensation Conversation
Chris mentioned paying advisors ~$1,000/month or similar.

**Standard advisor terms:**
- 0.1%–1% equity (options, 2-year vest, no cliff)
- Some cash stipend ($500–$2,000/month for active advisors)
- Clear deliverables: monthly meeting, introductions, reviewing pitch materials

**What advisors like Chris, Tyler, Cole do for you:**
- Validate your credibility to investors ("If Chris Johnson is advising them, they must be serious")
- Open doors (introductions are the most valuable thing in fundraising)
- Keep you on track technically and strategically

**How to compensate without cash early:**
- Advisory equity + monthly nominal cash once you have funding
- "We'd like to bring you on as an advisor at 0.5% on a 2-year vest. Once we close our seed round, we'd add a $1K/month stipend."

## The Fundraising Process Timeline (Realistic)
Chris said: "Budget 6–9 months to get initial capital in the door."

**Month 1–2:** Finalize deck, company structure, SAFE documents
**Month 2–3:** VIP outreach — warm intros, early believers, friends & strategic partners
**Month 3–4:** Formal investor meetings begin
**Month 4–6:** Follow-up meetings, due diligence requests, term discussions
**Month 6–9:** Close first tranche of SAFE round
**Ongoing:** Continue closing as you hit milestones
