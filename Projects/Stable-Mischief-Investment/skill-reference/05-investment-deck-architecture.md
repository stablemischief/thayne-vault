# Skill 05: Investment Deck Architecture
## Building the Deck Slide by Slide — Institutional Grade

---

## What Institutional Investors Actually Want
Before building a single slide, understand what's in their head:

1. **Is the exit clear?** — If I put $X in, how do I get $10X out, and when?
2. **Can this team execute?** — Do they have the experience and credibility to build this?
3. **Is the market real and big enough?** — Is this a billion-dollar prize or a science project?
4. **Do I understand what they're building?** — If I can't explain it to my LP committee, I won't fund it
5. **What's the risk?** — Technology, market, regulatory, team

**Everything in your deck is answering these 5 questions.**

---

## Two Types of Decks to Build

**1. The Teaser / Executive Summary (1–2 pages)**
- Used for cold outreach, email attachments, intro meetings
- High-level: who you are, what you do, the market, the ask
- Goal: get a meeting, not close a deal

**2. The Full Pitch Deck (12–18 slides)**
- Used in actual investor meetings
- You walk through it — it's a conversation aid, not a document
- Should be able to stand alone if sent digitally
- No paragraphs — bullet points, numbers, visuals
- Reference: Agrellus used 14–15 slides

## The Recommended Slide Structure

### Slide 1: Cover
- Company name, tagline, logo
- Your name + title
- Date
- "Confidential — Not for Distribution"
- Tagline should be crisp and memorable (Agrellus: "Connecting Acres for Your World")
- Stable Mischief candidate: something around "Known Intelligence for Agriculture" or "Building the AI Infrastructure for American Agriculture"

### Slide 2: The Problem
- What is broken in agriculture today?
- Make it visceral — show the paper, the legacy system, the green screen
- Reference: crop insurance PDF that Mark sent you — the smart PDF with dropdowns that someone probably prints and re-enters manually
- 3–5 bullet points max
- Frame: "Agriculture is where medicine was 30 years ago"

### Slide 3: The Solution
- What Stable Mischief does — one clear sentence at the top
- The platform concept: AI-native development company building vertical intelligence for agriculture
- 3–4 key capabilities (not features — capabilities)
- Keep it simple. Don't show the product yet.

### Slide 4: The Product(s) / What We Build
- This is where you show Agfin as proof of concept
- Show the product categories: underwriting AI, marketplace intelligence, operational tools, media/content
- Agrellus relationship: distribution partner and validation client
- Visual: product ecosystem diagram if possible

### Slide 5: Market Opportunity (TAM/SAM/SOM)
- Three clear numbers with sources
- The visual: concentric circles or bar chart
- Agriculture market size + ag-tech sub-market
- The "race" framing
- Geography expansion path

### Slide 6: Business Model / Revenue Streams
- How you make money — multiple streams = resilience
- For Stable Mischief: licensing, SaaS, white-label, usage-based, build+equity
- Show unit economics if you can: "A single bank licensing deal = $X ARR"
- The J-curve: once infrastructure is built, revenue scales without proportional cost increase

### Slide 7: Competitive Landscape
- The hardest slide — address it directly
- Use the comparison matrix approach: capabilities across the top, players down the side
- Frame: "We sit at the intersection of three existing markets. No one else covers all of this."
- Do NOT say "we have no competitors" — say "here's how the landscape is fragmented"

### Slide 8: Traction / Proof Points
- For pre-revenue: Agfin built in 6 months, 2 people
- Agrellus partnership and access to 1.5M farmers
- Multi-agent AI architecture deployed in production
- Nesvik client relationship + interest in investing
- Kowerk in development
- Dynamous community / advisor relationships

### Slide 9: TSR & Competitive Advantage
- The TSR repositioning story (from 5% ag commodity tier to 15–17% tech/financial services tier)
- Why this company is not an agriculture company
- What creates defensible moat: data assets, relationships, AI methodology, build speed

### Slide 10: Go-to-Market Strategy
- Phase 1: Agrellus partnership as beachhead — access to 1.5M farmers, proven use case
- Phase 2: License to financial institutions (banks, Farm Credit, insurance companies)
- Phase 3: Expand product suite and geographic reach
- The "who sells this" question: partnerships, direct sales, or platform distribution?

### Slide 11: Financial Projections (5-Year)
- Revenue by stream: licensing, SaaS, usage, build fees
- Simple chart: year 1–5 revenue
- Don't over-engineer — directionally correct, shows scale
- Reference Agrellus: $5.3M → $113.2M over 4 years as a comparable
- Show the J-curve: slow start, then inflection as platform matures

### Slide 12: Use of Funds
- Clear breakdown of how you'll spend the capital
- Must include market-rate salaries — this signals professionalism to institutional investors
- Categories: team/payroll, product development, sales/marketing, legal/compliance, operations
- "Here's exactly how this money turns into the company we described on slide 9"

### Slide 13: Team
- Photos, names, titles
- Key exits and credentials — Chris's involvement as advisor front and center
- James: product + AI orchestration leadership
- Mitch: full-stack technical execution
- Tristan (incoming): AI-native developer
- Advisors: Chris Johnson, Tyler (Dynamous), Cole (Dynamous)
- "This team has built and deployed production AI in agriculture. Here's the evidence."

### Slide 14: The Ask
- How much you're raising: "We are raising $X million"
- Structure: SAFE notes, Tier 1 at $X cap
- Use of funds (reference Slide 12)
- What this gets us to: "18 months of runway, 3 products deployed, $X ARR"
- The exit story in one sentence: "We are building for acquisition by a financial institution or strategic ag player at $X valuation within 5–7 years"

### Slide 15 (Optional): The Exit Story
- Who buys you and why
- Timeline
- Comparable acquisitions in adjacent spaces
- "Here's why this is a clear path to return for your investment"

## Design Principles
- Maximum 5 bullet points per slide
- One key message per slide (if you had to pull one thing from this slide, what is it?)
- Use visuals over text wherever possible — charts, diagrams, icons
- Consistent color scheme — professional, not flashy
- Font: clean, readable (Inter, Helvetica, similar)
- Numbers: always source them, even if just "(Source: USDA)" in fine print
- Appendix: put all the detail data — investors will ask for it in due diligence

---

## The "Leave Behind" vs. The "Walk Through" Deck
Your deck will be used in two modes:
1. **You present it** — your narrative fills in all the gaps. Slides are minimal.
2. **They read it alone** — it needs to stand alone with enough context.

Solution: Build the full deck first (for you to present), then create a slightly more annotated version with speaker notes or a short written narrative for leave-behind use.
