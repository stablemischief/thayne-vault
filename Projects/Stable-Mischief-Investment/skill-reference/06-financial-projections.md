# Skill 06: Financial Projections & Use of Funds
## Building Credible Numbers Without Existing Revenue

---

## The Purpose of Financial Projections in a Pitch
You're not trying to predict the future with precision.
You're showing investors:
1. You understand how the business makes money
2. You've thought through the path from zero to scale
3. The numbers are plausible given market size and your model
4. The return potential justifies their risk

"Directionally correct and intellectually honest" is the goal. Wild optimism destroys credibility. Conservative realism with a clear growth story builds it.

## Building the 5-Year Revenue Model

### Step 1: Identify Each Revenue Stream
For Stable Mischief (preliminary — refine with James):

| Stream | Description | Pricing Model |
|---|---|---|
| **Licensing** | AI underwriting engine / platform licensed to banks, insurers | Annual contract ($50K–$250K/year) |
| **SaaS (Kowerk)** | Business AI interaction platform | Monthly subscription ($X/seat or $X/month) |
| **White-label / Build** | Build custom AI tools for ag clients | Project fee + ongoing retainer |
| **Usage-based** | Per-document processed, per-loan underwritten | Volume pricing |
| **Data / Analytics** | Aggregated farm intelligence sold upstream | Subscription or per-report |
| **Consulting/Advisory** | Strategic AI implementation for ag enterprises | Retainer or project |

### Step 2: Build Assumptions Per Stream

**Licensing Example:**
- Year 1: 2 license deals × $75K avg = $150K
- Year 2: 8 deals × $85K avg = $680K
- Year 3: 20 deals × $100K avg = $2M
- Year 4: 45 deals × $110K avg = $4.95M
- Year 5: 80 deals × $125K avg = $10M

Each assumption should be defensible: "We have relationships with X institutions through the Agrellus network. Conversion of 2 in year 1 is conservative."

### Step 3: Build the J-Curve Story
The J-curve is your friend. It's honest and it's what good software businesses look like:

- Years 1–2: Investment phase. Revenue is low. We're building the platform and signing early clients.
- Year 3: Inflection point. Infrastructure is done. Each new client is nearly pure margin.
- Years 4–5: Scale. Revenue grows faster than costs.

**Reference:** Agrellus projects $5.3M in 2026 → $113.2M in 2030 = ~21x growth in 4 years. Your numbers should be more conservative at first but tell the same structural story.

## Use of Funds — The Most Important Slide for Institutional Investors

**Chris's core message:** Show competitive market-rate salaries. Bootstrapper mentality (no salaries) signals you can't attract talent. Institutional investors want to know: "Is this team set up to actually execute?"

### Use of Funds Categories

**1. Team / Payroll (largest category — typically 50–65% of raise)**
- James Whitfield (CEO): $X/year (competitive with startup CEO in his market)
- Mitch (CTO/Technical Lead): $X/year
- Tristan (AI-native Developer): $X/year (part-time initially → full-time)
- Anton (Dev, Europe): $X/year
- Future hires (sales, support): budgeted forward

Market rate reference (2025 startup context):
- Startup CEO: $120–180K/year depending on stage
- Senior dev in AI: $130–180K/year
- AI-native developer (emerging): $90–130K/year

**2. Product Development (15–20%)**
- Infrastructure / cloud costs (AWS, Anthropic, etc.)
- Dev tools and licenses
- QA and testing

**3. Sales & Marketing (10–15%)**
- Pitch deck design, collateral
- Conference attendance (ag-tech conferences)
- Early sales hires or contractors

**4. Legal & Compliance (5–8%)**
- Corporate formation (Baker Donelson or similar)
- SAFE/equity documentation
- Data privacy compliance
- IP protection

**5. Operations (5–10%)**
- Office / co-working space
- Travel (investor meetings, Agrellus partnership visits)
- Accounting / bookkeeping
- General admin

## What NOT to Do
- Don't show a hockey stick with no explanation of what drives the inflection
- Don't use round numbers throughout (looks lazy — $1M, $5M, $10M) — use modeled numbers
- Don't pad projections to match what you think investors want to hear
- Don't omit costs — show gross margin, not just revenue
- Don't forget to include your own salary — investors will ask "is James getting paid?" and the answer should be yes
