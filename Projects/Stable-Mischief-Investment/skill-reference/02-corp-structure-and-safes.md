# Skill 02: Corporate Structure & SAFE Notes
## How to Legally Structure the Company and Raise Early Capital

---

## Step 1: Form the Right Entity

**Always a C-Corporation (Delaware preferred)**
- Not an LLC — institutional investors typically require C-Corps
- Delaware is the standard jurisdiction even if you're based in Tennessee
- Why Delaware: investor-friendly laws, established case law, standard for VCs
- Cost: ~$500–1,500 to form + annual franchise tax (~$400/year minimum)

**Key elements to set up at formation:**
- Articles of Incorporation
- Bylaws
- Stock authorization (typically 10M–15M shares authorized at formation)
- Founders' stock issuance with vesting schedules
- IP assignment agreements (all IP built by founders assigned to the company)
- 83(b) election filing (within 30 days of stock grant — critical for tax purposes)

**Who to use:** A startup attorney (Baker Donelson, as Chris mentioned, or similar). Cost: $15–25K for full docs. Chris's advice: don't spend this until you have investment committed — use SAFEs first.

---

## Step 2: Founders' Equity & Vesting

**Standard founder vesting: 4-year vest, 1-year cliff**
- 1-year cliff: if a founder leaves before year 1, they get nothing
- After the cliff: monthly vesting for the remaining 3 years
- This protects all founders and signals maturity to investors

**Equity split considerations:**
- Who brought the idea? Who's doing the work? Who has the relationships?
- There is no perfect formula — but unequal splits that reflect reality are better than artificial 50/50s
- Advisors (like Chris): typically 0.1%–1% depending on engagement, on a 2-year vest with no cliff

**Equity pool for employees/team:**
- Set aside a 10–15% options pool at formation
- This pool is for Tristan, Anton, Sean, future hires
- Options (not stock) — they vest over time and have a strike price set at formation

## Step 3: SAFE Notes — The Tool Chris Described

**SAFE = Simple Agreement for Future Equity**
Created by Y Combinator. The standard early-stage fundraising instrument.

**How it works:**
- Investor gives you money NOW
- In exchange, they get equity LATER — at your next priced round — at a discount
- No valuation required at the time of investment
- No interest, no maturity date (standard YC post-money SAFE)
- No board seat, no control rights

**Why SAFEs are perfect for your situation:**
- You don't have to set a valuation yet
- Legal docs are simple (~5 pages, YC template is free)
- Closes fast — days, not months
- Investor gets a deal, you maintain control
- Sets up the "equity tester" Chris described — you're testing market appetite

---

## SAFE Key Terms to Know

**1. Valuation Cap**
The maximum valuation at which the SAFE converts to equity.
- Example: $10M cap SAFE — if your Series A is at $20M valuation, the SAFE investor converts as if they invested at $10M (they get twice the equity for the same money)
- This rewards early investors for taking early risk
- Chris's framework: set your realistic valuation at $50M, push to $75M. Your early SAFEs might have a $40–50M cap.

**2. Discount Rate**
A percentage discount on the price per share at conversion.
- Typical: 15–25% discount
- Example: if Series A share price is $1.00, a 20% discount SAFE investor pays $0.80 per share at conversion
- Some SAFEs have both a cap AND a discount — investor gets whichever is more favorable

**3. MFN (Most Favored Nation)**
If you issue future SAFEs with better terms, this SAFE automatically gets those better terms too.
- Common in uncapped SAFEs
- Protects early investors

## Tiered SAFE Structure (Chris's "Equity Tester" Model)

**Tier 1: VIP / Friends & Strategic Partners (Early)**
- Lower cap (more favorable to investor)
- Higher discount (20–25%)
- Reserved for: Adam/Nesvik, Chris personally, other early believers
- These people help you raise the next tier by talking about it

**Tier 2: Institutional / Larger Investors (Later)**
- Higher cap
- Standard discount (15–20%)
- These investors come in seeing that Tier 1 is full or nearly full — creates urgency

**The Sales Dynamic:**
"We have a Tier 1 SAFE round at $X cap — there's limited room. If you want to come in at these terms, now is the time. Tier 2 will be at higher valuation."

This is not manipulation — it's structure. It creates a legitimate reason for urgency and gives early believers a real reward.

## Cap Table Basics

A cap table (capitalization table) shows who owns what percentage of the company.

**At formation (example):**
| Party | Shares | % |
|---|---|---|
| James Whitfield | 4,500,000 | 45% |
| Mitch (co-founder) | 3,000,000 | 30% |
| Options Pool | 1,500,000 | 15% |
| Reserved | 1,000,000 | 10% |
| **Total** | **10,000,000** | **100%** |

**After SAFE round (pre-money, before conversion):**
SAFEs don't immediately dilute — they convert at the next priced round. But you should model the dilution to understand what you're giving up.

**Tools to manage cap table:** Carta (industry standard), or a simple spreadsheet early on.

---

## What NOT to Do
- Don't give equity informally ("I'll give you 5%" with a handshake)
- Don't skip the 83(b) election — costly tax mistake if you miss the 30-day window
- Don't use an LLC if you plan to raise institutional capital
- Don't give advisors equity without a vesting agreement
- Don't set up equity before you know who all the founders are
