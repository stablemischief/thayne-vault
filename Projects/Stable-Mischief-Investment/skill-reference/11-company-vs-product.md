# Skill 11: Company vs. Product — The Most Important Distinction
## You're Not Raising for a Feature. You're Raising for a Company.

---

## The Trap Most Founders Fall Into

The most common mistake early-stage founders make when pitching:

"We're raising money for our app / platform / product."

This positions you as a single-product bet. Investors hear:
- What happens if this product doesn't work?
- What's the second act?
- Are you a company or a feature?

Chris made this point explicitly in your meeting:
"We're funding the market with a bunch of tools... that's what makes it valuable."

---

## The Microsoft/Windows Analogy (From Your Conversation)

James said it himself: "You go back to 2002 and you're selling somebody, you gotta have a website... tech people are on the Internet."

More precisely — think about Microsoft in 1986:
- They weren't raising for Windows. They were raising for **Microsoft** — a software company.
- Windows was the proof-of-concept, the flagship product, the thing that opened doors.
- But the *company* was the bet. And Microsoft went on to build Office, Azure, Xbox, Teams.

**Stable Mischief = the company. Agfin, Kowerk, and future products = the portfolio.**

When you pitch, you're asking investors to bet on the company and its capacity to build valuable products — not to bet on any single product surviving.

---

## How to Frame It in the Pitch

**Wrong (product framing):**
"We built Agfin — an AI-powered crop finance underwriting platform. We're raising $5M to grow Agfin."

**Right (company framing):**
"We are building the AI infrastructure company for agriculture. Our first deployed product, Agfin, proves we can build complex, production-ready AI tools for this market in months with a small team. We're raising to scale the team and build the product suite this market needs."

The shift: Agfin is **evidence** of what the company can do. It's not the whole bet.

## The "Agrellus as Partner, Not Investor" Distinction

Chris was explicit: "Agrellus would not be an investor with this — I think it would be an absolutely spectacular partner."

**Why this matters:**
- Agrellus is a client/distribution partner, not a shareholder
- This keeps your cap table clean
- It means you're not beholden to Agrellus's direction
- But you leverage Agrellus for: market access, validation, introductions, distribution

**How to say it in the pitch:**
"Our strategic partnership with Agrellus gives us access to 1.5 million farmers and a validated market from day one. It's a distribution relationship, not an equity relationship — which means we're aligned on value creation without competing interests in the boardroom."

---

## The Services vs. Products Distinction

One thing to be careful about: **investors don't fund service businesses.**

If Stable Mischief looks like "we build AI things for clients," that's a consulting firm. Consulting firms don't get institutional investment — they get project fees.

**The distinction:**
- **Services:** We build custom AI tools for clients on a project basis (revenue = labor hours)
- **Products:** We build AI platforms that clients license, subscribe to, or use on usage-based pricing (revenue = recurring, scales without proportional headcount)

**Your positioning must lean hard toward products:**
- Agfin is a product, not a custom build. (Even though Agrellus was the first client, the platform is replicable.)
- Kowerk is a product.
- The AI underwriting engine can be licensed as a product to many institutions.

**The "build + equity" model Chris mentioned** is a hybrid that works for strategic partnerships — but in the pitch, lead with the product story.

## Practical Framing Exercise

When explaining Stable Mischief to an investor, finish this sentence:

"We are the _________ of agriculture."

Options:
- "We are the **Veeva** of agriculture" (Veeva = pharma vertical SaaS, $40B+ market cap)
- "We are the **Blend** of agriculture" (Blend = digital lending platform, acquired by major banks)
- "We are the **nCino** of agriculture" (nCino = bank operating system, IPO'd at $2B+)

Pick one that resonates and use it. Investors understand comps instantly. It anchors the conversation in a framework they already trust.
