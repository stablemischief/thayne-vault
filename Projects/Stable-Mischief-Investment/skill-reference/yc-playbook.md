## Part 1: When and Why to Raise

**Raise when you can tell a compelling story AND investors will be persuaded the opportunity is real.**
For most companies that means: a product they can see, a market they believe in, and some evidence of traction or a credible path to it.

**The Stable Mischief situation:** AgFin is a real, deployed product. The market is documented. The team has credibility. The advisor (Chris Johnson) has exits. This is fundable now.

**One critical rule from Paul Graham:** "Be in fundraising mode or not."
- Fundraising is so distracting it stops everything else. When you start, go all in and finish fast.
- Don't casually meet with investors when not in fundraising mode — it burns relationships and time.
- Corollary: once James commits to launching the raise, block time specifically for it. Don't mix it with client work.

## Part 2: How Much to Raise

**YC's framework:**
- Raise enough to reach your next fundable milestone (typically 12–18 months of runway)
- The amount you ask for must be tied to a believable plan
- Avoid giving up more than 20–25% in a seed round
- Multiple plans at different funding levels is smart — shows you've thought it through

**For Stable Mischief:**
- Target: $7.5M (following Chris's 2.5x guidance on actual operational need)
- This is on the higher end of seed rounds — justified by the Farm Credit market target, not just community bank market
- Tie it explicitly to milestones: X months of runway, Y product deployments, Z ARR target

**YC rule:** "Raise as much money as you need to reach profitability, so you'll never have to raise again. If you can't, raise enough to reach your next fundable milestone."

## Part 3: SAFEs — YC's Preferred Instrument

YC created the SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity) specifically to replace convertible notes. It is now the standard for early-stage fundraising.

**Key points:**
- No interest rate, no maturity date, no repayment requirement
- Negotiable terms: amount, cap, discount
- Converts to equity at the next priced round
- Converts at whichever is more favorable to the investor: the cap OR the discount

**The Post-Money SAFE (YC's current standard):**
- Investor knows exactly what percentage they're buying at the moment of investment
- Formula: Investment Amount ÷ Post-Money Valuation Cap = ownership %
- Example: $500K into a $10M cap SAFE = 5% ownership (before dilution from future raises)

**Why it matters for Stable Mischief:**
- Use SAFEs for the VIP/early investor tier (Chris, Adam/Nesvik, etc.)
- Set a cap that reflects the "pushed" valuation (~$50–75M per Chris's framework)
- Early investors get their reward through the cap; institutional investors come in at a higher cap later

**YC caution:** Use standard YC SAFE documents. "Well-known financing documents like YC's safe are well understood by the investor community and have been drafted to be fair, yet founder friendly."

## Part 4: The Fundraising Process — Paul Graham's Rules

### Rule 1: Be in fundraising mode or not
Don't casually talk to investors outside of a defined fundraising sprint. It creates false signals and distracts.

### Rule 2: Get introductions
"In phase 2, yes — you have to be introduced." Cold outreach to VCs doesn't work.
- Best intro: from an investor who has already committed to you
- Second best: from a founder of a company they've funded
- Chris Johnson's network is your most valuable asset here

### Rule 3: Treat investors as saying no until they say yes
"Investors prefer to wait if they can... they lead you on." Don't count verbal enthusiasm as commitment. A definite offer with no contingencies is a yes. Everything else is a no.

### Rule 4: Talk to investors in parallel, not serially
Running conversations simultaneously creates competitive pressure. Serial conversations let each investor wait indefinitely.

### Rule 5: Get the first commitment — it unlocks everything else
"Getting the first substantial offer can be half the total difficulty of fundraising." The first investor makes every other investor more comfortable. Target the most likely-to-say-yes investor first.

### Rule 6: Know exactly where you stand after every meeting
Before leaving any investor meeting: "What happens next? What do you need to decide? How long will that take?" Investors who are serious will answer. Those who aren't will be vague.

### Rule 7: Close investors as fast as possible once they commit
"The speed at which you can close investors once they've said yes is a signal of your competence." Don't let committed investors sit.

## Part 5: The Pitch — What Investors Are Actually Evaluating

**YC's framework:** Investors are evaluating three things:
1. **Is the idea compelling?** (Market + problem)
2. **Can this team execute?** (Founder credibility + relevant experience)
3. **Is the opportunity real and large enough?** (Market size + timing)

**The meeting dynamic:**
- Goal of first meeting: get a second meeting, not close
- If you can get the investor talking more than you — your odds increase dramatically
- Strike balance between confidence and humility. Never arrogance, never pushover.
- Connect personally: investors make long-term bets on people, not just companies

**Pitching is a skill — practice is the only path to good.** "There is no substitute for an extraordinary amount of practice."

## Part 6: Product/Market Fit — What It Is and Isn't

**Michael Seibel (YC Managing Director):** "Founders often try to signal product/market fit by pointing to employees or a funding round. What I look for is a frantic founding team trying to deal with ever-growing numbers of happy, loyal, and ideally paying customers."

**The hair on fire test (Sequoia/YC):** Find problems so painful that users will try even a half-built solution. A farmer waiting weeks for a loan decision while manually compiling documents — that is a hair-on-fire problem.

**For Stable Mischief before the raise:**
- AgFin is built. The question investors will ask: is anyone using it? Is it solving the problem as described?
- The strongest pre-raise move: get at least one real lender running documents through AgFin and able to speak to the time savings. That is proof of product/market fit.

**YC principle:** "The market pulls product out of the startup." The problem is the real opportunity. The first version of the solution is usually wrong and only through real users do you find what works. AgFin V1 is the beginning of this loop, not the end.

## Part 7: Valuation — YC's Honest Framework

**YC's take:** "There can only be the most notional sort of justification for any value at all. Let the market set your price."

**Practical guidance:**
- Seed valuations typically range from $2M to $20M+ (higher end for experienced teams)
- The goal is NOT to achieve the highest possible valuation — it's to find a number investors find attractive enough to write a check
- A high valuation that kills the deal is worse than a fair valuation that closes it
- That said: don't under-optimize. "The objective is to find a valuation with which you are comfortable, that will allow you to raise the amount you need with acceptable dilution."

**Dilution to avoid:** More than 20–25% in a seed round. If you're raising $7.5M and want to give up 15%, you need a $50M post-money valuation. If you're targeting $75M, you're giving up 10%.

## Part 8: What Makes Investors Write Checks

From Paul Graham and YC experience — the real factors:

1. **Other investors have committed.** Herd behavior is real. The first check is the hardest. Every subsequent check is easier.
2. **The founder is clearly the right person for this problem.** Expertise + passion + relevant experience.
3. **The market is obviously large.** Not debatable.
4. **The timing is right.** Why is this the moment? (For Stable Mischief: agentic AI has hit an inflection point. Agriculture is 30 years behind on technology adoption. The window is open.)
5. **There is some evidence the thing works.** Not necessarily revenue — could be a deployed product, a LOI, a design partner, a pilot.

## Part 10: Michael Seibel — How to Pitch Your Seed Stage Startup
**Source:** Transcript provided by James, March 2026

### The Core Principle
"You stand out by being concise and easy to understand — not by energy, pizzazz, or shark tankiness."

Seibel has done 2,000+ YC interviews. His #1 finding: if he doesn't know what your company does, he can't fund you. The barrier is almost always the founder — not the investor, not the market.

### The 6 Elements of a Pitch (In This Order)

**1. What do you do**
Two sentences + one specific example. That's it.
- Sentence 1: What the company does and for whom
- Sentence 2: How it makes money / the mechanism
- Example: specific, concrete, makes the problem and solution vivid

The Airbnb model:
- "Airbnb lets any home or apartment owner rent out their apartment online."
- "They collect payment online and take a 15% fee for every booking."
- Example: "Imagine you're a waiter in DC during the Obama inauguration. Every hotel room is booked. You can make $2–4K renting your apartment out — enough for 2–3 months of rent. Just take photos and post a listing. Airbnb handles the payments."

**Common mistakes:**
- Using customer language that investors don't understand (your website copy is for customers, not investors)
- Trying to be 100% accurate instead of 100% clear (goal: 80% accurate, 100% clear)
- Vague examples that don't paint a picture
- Leaving the first slide without investors knowing what you do

**For Stable Mischief:** Never leave the opening without the investor understanding: *AgFin replaces weeks of manual document entry in agricultural lending with AI-powered extraction. What took analysts weeks now takes hours.*

---

**2. Team**
Who's on the team, what do they do, what are their specific accomplishments, and — most importantly — how have they personally experienced the problem.

"If you've done something cool, say it. Without the life story. Just say it."

Mars rover example: founder worked on it, never mentioned it. Seibel found out in office hours. Don't do that.

**For Stable Mischief:**
- James: 26 years in tech, co-founded Firestone Direct (grew $0→$19M), personal farmer
- Mitch: VP at Sirius XM, product leadership at Verizon Wireless, hands-on builder
- Weave in the personal ag connection — both founders farm. That's not irrelevant color. That's why they're the right people for this problem.
- Chris Johnson: advisor. **Important Seibel note: "Nobody cares about your advisors."** He means on the team slide. Advisors are mentioned, not featured. Chris's value is in the relationship and deal network, not on a slide.

---

**3. Traction**
Clear explanation of what you've accomplished since you started — and include the TIME it took.

"If you built an iOS app and it's in TestFlight with 100 users in one month — that's impressive. If it's been 2 years and it's in TestFlight with 100 users — that's not."

Investors are looking for **momentum** — do you get things done quickly? — not just revenue or users.

**Only use a graph if it goes up and to the right.** A bad traction slide is worse than no traction slide.

**For Stable Mischief:**
- AgFin: concept to working production platform in under 90 days, team of fewer than 5
- That IS traction — it shows execution velocity, which is exactly what investors are looking for in a pre-revenue company

**Don't include:**
- Advisor relationships as traction
- LOIs that haven't signed
- "Survey results show interest"

---

**4. Unique Insights**
The non-obvious things you've learned about the problem, the customer, or the solution.

"This is the most intellectually interesting part of a pitch — but you only get the right to do this if I know what your company does and I'm impressed with your team."

Your insight isn't unique if 50%+ of the room would agree with it when you say it. It needs to be something most people would miss or disagree with.

**For Stable Mischief:**
- The compliance problem isn't just speed — it's auditability. Banks that adopt AI document extraction without an immutable audit trail will face regulatory rejection. That's why we built the audit trail into the architecture from day one, not as a feature added later.
- The document gap exists because every competitor approached this from the workflow side (how do we route loans) rather than the document intelligence side (how do we extract and certify data). We went document-first. That's why we have a provisional patent and they don't.
- Agriculture's document problem is more complex than any other lending vertical because of the FSA-state structure and multi-entity farm operations. You can't just lift a fintech lending product and drop it in. It requires domain-specific architecture.

---

**5. Market Size**
"How you calculate the number is more important than the number itself."

Show the math. Bottom-up: how many potential customers × how much you'll charge them × why they'll pay that. This teaches the investor — and investors fund people they think of as experts.

**Common mistakes:**
- Quoting a market research report without showing how you fit in it ("JP Morgan says the market is $120T..." — nobody cares)
- Not showing the math
- Not referencing comparable products and their pricing

**For Stable Mischief:** Use the specific market numbers from research (Farm Credit System $457B, 300,000+ ag loans/year, etc.) with a clear formula showing how AgFin monetizes a fraction of that.

---

**6. The Ask**
"You would be shocked at how many pitches people do and they don't ask for money."

Seibel estimates 70% of pitches end without ever asking. The founder asks for questions, investor says no questions, meeting ends, no money was ever requested.

**Seibel's 3 investor mindsets:**
1. I will never fund this company (most common)
2. I will definitely fund this company (very rare)
3. If they ask me it will be uncomfortable to say no, so I'll write a check (up to 10% of the time)

**You only access category 3 by asking.** Put them on the spot. This is especially true for angels.

**What to include in the ask slide:**
- How much you're raising
- Social proof (who else has invested, how much is already committed)
- What milestone you'll hit with this money in 18–24 months (not who you'll hire — that's a means, not an end)

**What NOT to include:**
- A list of future hires (nobody cares)
- Vague use of funds language ("for engineering and marketing")

---

### Overall Pitch Principles (Seibel)

**Order points from most to least impressive** after the opening "what do you do." If traction is your best thing, put it second. If team is your best thing, put it second.

**Earn every 2 minutes.** Especially on Zoom. If the last 2 minutes sucked, the investor is checking email. Don't save the good stuff for the end.

**It's not a book report.** Draw them into conversation. If the investor is talking 50%+ of the time, they're convincing themselves to invest. The more they contribute ideas, the more they talk themselves into it. If they show interest in something — run with it, skip to that slide.

**Watch their face.** Investors are not trained poker players. Their tells are visible. Pay attention.

**Visually boring slides are better than visually interesting ones.** You want them looking at you, not the designer's work. If you need a designer to make your deck, you're doing it wrong.

## Part 11: Aaron Harris — How to Build Your Seed Round Pitch Deck
**Source:** Screenshots of YC article provided by James, March 2026

### Key Principle
"Focus on narrative. The rest is commentary."

The deck is a template for the story. Most of the meaning comes from how you tell it, not from the slides themselves.

### The Slide-by-Slide Structure

**1. Title slide**
Company name + one line description of what you do. Nothing else.

**2. Problem**
State clearly. How does this problem impact real people and businesses? Particulars are valuable.

**3. Solution**
What you do, in as few words as possible. Concrete benefits. Don't explain how it works — explain what it does.

**4. Traction** (if you have it)
Show it. Only use a graph if it goes up and to the right. Add context stats alongside the chart. "Revenue would be better here, but this is ok."

**5. Unique insights / Why you win**
What makes you special, what your insights are. "Tell the investor what makes you so special, what makes this work, what your insights are. This might take more than one slide."

**6. Business model**
How you make money. "You probably don't know all the details yet, but you should know a lot of them. If you need more space to dig into something complicated, add slides."

**7. Market / Future growth**
Is it going to be big? Will you make it big? How much will you make off this? Convince the investor they're going to make money with you.

**8. Team**
What makes the team particularly well suited to the problem. "This should be about founders. Nobody cares about your advisors."

**9. Ask**
How much you need, what it's for, what milestones it gets you to. "If you can lay out where you'll be inside of a year, which should make you Series A ready — that's powerful."

### The Design Note
The deck is intentionally simple. Kevin Hale (YC partner) wrote a separate piece on how to design decks well — see that for design guidance. But the narrative structure above is the foundation.

## Part 12: Dalton Caldwell — Investor Meeting Process
**Source:** Transcript provided by James (Startup Investor School Day 2), March 2026

*Note: This talk is from the investor's perspective — useful for James to understand how investors evaluate him.*

### What Investors Are Looking For in a Meeting
Always starts with basics:
- What does your company do?
- Who's on the team?
- What progress have you made?
- How much have you raised and on what terms?

"Hear the founder tell their own story in their own words. Forget everything you already know from the deck."

### The Conviction Test
After every meeting, ask yourself:
- Am I more or less convinced the more I talk to the founder?
- Do I trust them? Even if they say all the right things — does my gut say they're being straight with me?
- Would I want to work with/for this company?
- Can I imagine them running a large company someday?

### Signals James Should Watch For
- Investor who is vague about next steps = not interested
- "I'm in once you find the rest of the round" = actually a no
- Meeting after meeting with no commitment = no
- "Treat investors as saying no until they say yes in the form of a definite offer with no contingencies"

### One Rule That Applies to James
"If you're not a yes, you are a no."

Don't leave investor conversations in ambiguous land. At the end of each meeting, know where you stand. Ask directly: "What would you need to see to make a decision?"

## Part 13: Kevin Hale — How to Design a Better Pitch Deck
**Source:** Screenshot of YC article provided by James, March 2026

### The Three Design Principles
Everything in Kevin Hale's deck design framework comes down to three words:

**Make it legible. Make it simple. Make it obvious.**

---

### Legibility
"Legible slides are ones that people sitting in the back row with bad eyesight can read."

If you can't manage anything else: **Helvetica, Bold, 100pt minimum.**

Most founders pack too much onto slides. The result is that nobody reads any of it. A slide that can be read from 30 feet away will always outperform a slide that requires leaning in.

**For Stable Mischief:** If a slide needs a paragraph of explanation text to make sense, it hasn't been reduced to its core message yet. Reduce further.

---

### Simplicity
"Simple ideas are ones that aren't interleaved with other ideas."

One slide = one idea. When you try to communicate two things on the same slide, the audience understands neither.

This is the hardest discipline for founders because they want to show everything they know. Resist it. Each slide gets one job.

**The test:** What is the single sentence this slide is trying to say? If you can't answer that in 5 seconds, the slide isn't done yet.

---

### Obviousness
"Obvious slides are ones that can be understood at a glance."

If someone needs to study a slide to understand it, it failed. The message should land in under 3 seconds.

For charts and graphs:
- One trend, clearly labeled
- The key number displayed prominently (50% growth = show "50%" in large type, not buried in the axis)
- No unnecessary data series or chart junk
- The takeaway is the headline, not something the viewer has to infer

**Bad chart:** Multiple overlapping lines, small axis labels, no labeled data points, title says "Revenue Over Time"
**Good chart:** Single line going up and to the right, key milestone labeled, title says "Revenue 3x'd in 6 Months"

---

### The Bottom Line
"Investors invest in teams, not slides."

The deck is a support structure for the story the founder tells. Its job is to not distract. Simple, legible, obvious slides keep attention on the founder — where it belongs.

This is why Seibel says visually boring is better than visually interesting. Hale's framework explains why technically: every visual element that's more interesting than necessary is pulling attention away from the person investors are actually evaluating.

---

### Practical Application for Stable Mischief Deck
- Font: Inter Bold or Helvetica Bold, 36pt minimum body, 60pt+ for key numbers
- Colors: Stable Mischief brand palette (see brand-identity.md skill), not default PowerPoint
- Each slide: one message, stated as a headline at the top in plain English
- Charts: one metric per chart, key number called out explicitly
- No bullet points with 6+ items — if you have 6 bullets, you have 2 slides
- No paragraphs of text — if it requires a paragraph, it belongs in the leave-behind document, not the deck
