# Skill 00: Investment Copywriting Voice
## How to Write Content That Sounds Like a Credible Founder — Not an AI

---

## The Core Problem This Skill Solves

AI-generated investment content has a recognizable signature:
- Superlatives without evidence ("fastest," "most powerful," "revolutionary")
- Passive constructions that avoid accountability ("it has been demonstrated that...")
- Abstract nouns stacked on top of each other ("synergistic ecosystem of transformative solutions")
- Hedged overclaiming ("potentially one of the most significant opportunities in...")
- Bullet lists that restate the obvious
- Excitement without specificity

Institutional investors read hundreds of decks. They recognize this voice immediately and discount it. The goal is to write like a founder who knows what they built, knows the market, and doesn't need to oversell.

---

## The Voice We're Writing In

**Model:** A confident, experienced operator who has done hard things and isn't performing enthusiasm. Someone who spent 26 years in technology, built a $19M business inside a Fortune 500, started a small farm, and found an underserved market. Someone who talks to farmers and bankers and knows the difference between a crop lien and a Schedule F.

**Tone characteristics:**
- Direct. Says what it means on the first try.
- Specific. Uses numbers, names, and concrete examples instead of adjectives.
- Grounded. Claims only what can be supported.
- Confident without being arrogant. Doesn't need exclamation points.
- Occasionally human. A phrase that sounds like something a real person would say is better than a phrase that sounds polished.

---

## Rules for Every Sentence

**Rule 1: No unsupported superlatives.**
Wrong: "Stable Mischief is building the most advanced agricultural AI platform in the market."
Right: "Stable Mischief built a working AI underwriting system for agricultural lending in less than 90 days, with a small team."

**Rule 2: Specificity over abstraction.**
Wrong: "Agriculture represents a massive and largely untapped market opportunity."
Right: "U.S. banks held $205 billion in farm loans at year-end 2024. More than half of ag lenders only began digitizing in 2025."

**Rule 3: Earn the claim before you make it.**
Wrong: "Stable Mischief's technology is differentiated from every competitor."
Right: "No competitor currently combines multi-agent extraction, analyst review workflow, and compliance-grade audit trails in a single platform. Landjourney handles document collection. iBusiness/LenderAI handles workflow automation. Neither does what AgFin does."

**Rule 4: Avoid "we believe" and "we think" unless followed immediately by evidence.**
Wrong: "We believe this market is ready for disruption."
Right: "Landjourney raised $4.5M in seed funding in 2025 and is already raising a Series A. The market is moving."

**Rule 5: One idea per sentence.**
Long compound sentences with multiple claims buried inside them lose readers. Break them apart.

**Rule 6: Use active voice.**
Wrong: "A solution has been developed that addresses the inefficiencies present in current agricultural lending workflows."
Right: "AgFin replaces manual document entry with AI-powered extraction. What took analysts weeks now takes hours."

**Rule 7: Don't explain what investors already know.**
Wrong: "Agriculture is a critical sector of the global economy that feeds billions of people worldwide."
Right: Start with what's broken about it, not what it is.

## Vocabulary to Use and Avoid

### Use
- "Agentic" / "multi-agent" (specific technical term, not a buzzword in this context)
- "Compliance-grade" (specific claim about a real regulatory requirement)
- "Document intelligence" (descriptive and accurate)
- "Ag lending" / "crop finance" (industry terms that signal domain knowledge)
- "Farm Credit System" / "AIPs" / "FSA-578" (specific vocabulary signals credibility)
- "Provisional patent" (concrete IP claim)
- "Immutable audit trail" (technical term with regulatory meaning)
- Revenue per acre (farmer language — shows you know who you're serving)

### Avoid
- "AI-powered" as a standalone descriptor (everyone says this)
- "Revolutionary" / "transformative" / "game-changing"
- "Disruptive" (overused to meaninglessness)
- "Leverage" as a verb (corporate speak)
- "Solution" as a noun (everything is a "solution" — say what it actually does)
- "Space" as a noun ("the ag space") — say "market" or "industry"
- "Ecosystem" unless describing something with genuine interdependence
- "World-class" / "best-in-class"
- "Passionate about" (founders are supposed to be — it's not a differentiator)

## How to Write About Competitors

The instinct is to minimize or ignore them. Don't.

Naming competitors and explaining specifically how you're different is more credible than claiming empty white space. It signals that you actually know the market.

**Template:**
"[Competitor] exists and has [X funding / X customers]. They handle [specific function]. They do not have [specific capability we have]. That gap is where AgFin operates."

Landjourney is the specific example to use: seed-funded 2025, already back for Series A, handles document collection and loan matching. Does not have multi-agent extraction with field-level arbitration and immutable audit trails.

---

## How to Write About the Team

Don't list credentials as bullet points. Tell it as a through-line.

Wrong:
- James Whitfield: 26 years technology experience, MBA Vanderbilt, co-founder Firestone Direct
- Mitch Dornich: VP Product at Sirius XM, VP at Verizon Wireless

Right:
"James Whitfield spent 26 years in technology before co-founding Firestone Direct inside Bridgestone Americas, growing it from zero to $19M in three years. He runs a small farm in Hendersonville, Tennessee. Mitch Dornich ran product divisions at Verizon Wireless and Sirius XM before retiring and then deciding — after meeting James at an AI bootcamp — that agriculture technology was worth coming back for."

The goal: make them sound like real people with a real reason for being here.

---

## How to Write About the Market

Lead with what's broken, not with market size numbers.

**Wrong order:**
1. Here is the market size ($342B TAM...)
2. Here is the problem (agriculture is legacy...)
3. Here is our solution

**Right order:**
1. Here is what's broken (a farmer with 60 farm locations waits weeks for a loan decision while a bank analyst manually keys data from printed PDFs)
2. Here is why (agriculture runs on systems built before the internet was a business tool)
3. Here is what changed (agentic AI can now replace that workflow end-to-end)
4. Here is the scale of the opportunity (the numbers)

The human problem before the market size. Always.

---

## Sentences to Test Your Writing Against

Read your draft aloud. If any sentence makes you think "that sounds like a press release," rewrite it.

Ask: Would a farmer understand this? Would a banker believe this? Would a VC have heard this exact sentence in three other decks this week?

If the answer to that last question is yes — rewrite it.

## The Stable Mischief Brand Voice (Specific to This Company)

The name "Stable Mischief" is deliberately unexpected. It signals that this is not a corporate entity that emerged from a consulting firm. It has character.

The narrative should carry that same energy: professional but not sterile, confident but not boastful, specific but not jargon-heavy.

The "barn of solutions" / "stable of products" framing is exactly right — it's agricultural metaphor used intentionally, not accidentally. Use it. It's memorable and it's theirs.

The mission statement — "helping farmers generate and keep more revenue per acre" — is plain English. Write everything else at that same level of plainness.
